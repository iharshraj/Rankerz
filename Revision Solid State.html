    Amorphous and crystalline solids

  • Amorphous are isotropic and crystalline are Anisotropic.

  • Crystallines have regular and consistent physical properties.

  • Amorphoes (eg, glass, plastics) can flow but very slowly.

  • There are four types of crystalline solids: Molecular , Ionic , Metallic and Covalent(or Network) Solids.

  • Ionic solids form crystallines and they are bad conductors
    of electricity (NaCl, in solid form) and they are brittle.

  • Metallic solids > Ionic Solids (for boiling and Melting Point) because Metallic solids
    are held together with strong multi-directional electrostatic forces.

  • In sample form ,Covalent Solids > Ionic Solids (for b.p and m.p) because ionic solids are present in blocks of lattices
    which are easier to seperate than covalent solids which are held together with intermingled strong multi-directional covalent bonds

  • In individual lattice form, Covalent Solids < Ionic Solids (for b.p and m.p) because Electrostatics force > Covalent

    • 3-d lattices



  • There are total 14 possible 3d lattices and they are known as Bravais Lattices.

  • Primitive cells are lattices in which lattice points are vertices of structure only.

  • Non-primitive cells are lattices in which lattice points are present at places other than vertices of structure too.
    They are of 3 types :
    (i) BCC (body centered)
    (ii) FCC (Face centered), they are very common form due to high packing efficiency factor.
    (iii)End Centered ,they are mostly present When a,b and c (side lengths) are not equal.

  • Structures and their forms :-
    Cubic: Simple(primitive) , BCC , FCC
    Tetragonal : Simple , BCC
    Rhombohedral : Simple , BCC , FCC ,End Centered
    Monoclinic : Simple , End centered
    Triclinic : Simple
    Hexagonal : Simple
    (Note that total number of forms = 14)

  • Packing efficiency= [Vol.(or area) occupied by atoms inside the cell] / [Vol.(or area) of unit cell]
    FCC and HCP have highest Packing eff. about 74%

  • Vol. of HCP(Hexagonal lattice)= 24√2*(r)3 , where "r" is radius of the atom.

  • Cordination Number(C.N)= No. of nearest surrounding atoms for any atom
    for C.N , first select any convinient lattice point then
    C.N= (n*p)/k , where
    n = number of nearby atoms (anions) for the selected atom in a lattice
    p = potion of that atom in that cell (eg , for vertex lattice points p=1/8)
    k = number of cells it takes to completely cover the line segment that joins selected atom and its nearest neighbour.

  • C.N for , Simple cell = 6 , BCC = 8 , FCC = 12

    • ABAB and ABCABC packing


  • ABAB packing is shown by HCP and ABCABC is shown by FCC

    • Voids

  • Types of void :
    Trigonal Void (present only in 2d lattices)
    Tetrahedral Void , equals to 2z (where, z = no. of complete atoms in a cell)
    Octahedral Void , equals to z
    Body Centered Void , only in BCC and atmost 1.

  • FCC = 4 oct and 8 tetra Voids
    BCC = 2 oct and 4 tetra Voids
    HCP = 6 oct and 12 tetra Voids

  • Conditions for creation of voids , r+ means radius of cation and r- anion
    0.155 ≤ r+⁄r- < 0.225 , for Trigonal
    0.255 ≤ r+⁄r- < 0.414 , for Tetrahedral
    0.414 ≤ r+⁄r- < 0.732 , for Octahedral
    0.732 ≤ r+⁄r- < 1.000 , for Body Centered

    • Types of Ionic Solids

  • NaCl Type: Cations(+) fills all Octahedral positions in FCC
    z = 4 (net atoms in one cell)
    dist of Closest cation and anion = a⁄2 (a=edge length)
    r+ ≈ r-⁄2

  • ZnS Type : Cations(+) fill half of tetrahedral voids in FCC(in alternate form)
    z = 4 , Closest dist b/w cation and anion is a√3⁄4

  • CsCl Type : Cation occupied Body Centered Void in BCC
    z = 1 , Closest dist b/w cation and anion is (a√3)⁄2
    r+ ≈ r-

  • CaF2 Type : In these Fluorite structures (r+ > r-) (opp. of norm) , so cations(+) occupies lattice points instead.
    and anions(-) occupies all tetrahedral voids in the lattice of FCC.
    C.N of F- = 4 and , Ca2+ = 8

  • Na2O Type : Its normal , Na+ will fill all tetrahedral voids in FCC lattice.
    C.N of Na+ = 4 and , O2- = 8

    • Defects in Solids


  • Defects in solids are of 2 types:- (a)Point defect and, (b)Line defect
    (a)Point defect : They are irregularities of points or atom arrangements in a cell.
    (b)Line defect : They are irregularities in group of points in a substance.

  • Point defects are of 3 types :
    (i) Stoichiometric defect: ratio between cations and anions remains same
    (ii) Impurity defect : Mixing of other elements in the solid (eg, duping)
    (iii) Non-stoichiometric defect : ratio between cations and anions changes

  • Stoichiometric defects can happen 2 ways :
    1) Vacancy defect : a lattice point being left vacant , this decreases density.It can occur when a subst. is heated.
    2) Interstitial defect : when an atom leaves their places and occupies interstitial spaces , this increases density.

  • Ionic solids when undergoes stoichiometric defect they have to make sure the net neutrality of substance.
    They can undergo stoichiometric defect in 2 ways :-
    (a) Schottky defect : Its a type of vacancy defect in Ionic Solids , in this defect cations and anions leave their sites in pair
    ,this decreases density of substance.It occurs when there not a significant difference in ionic radius of cations and anions.
    Examples of solids showing this defect : NaCl , AgBr(common in both defects) , KCl, NaBr
    r+ ≈ r-

    (b) Frenkel defect : Its a type of Interstitial defect in Ionic solids , in this cations (mostly) leaves their lattice points
    and occupies voids , it occurs when difference between size of cations and anions is quite large and cations
    cannot take this difference and they migrate.
    This doesnt changes density.Its also called dislocation defect.
    Examples of solids showing this defect: ZnS, AgCl, AgBr, and AgI
    r+ << r-



  • Non-Stoichiometric defect have two types:-
    (a) Metal excess defect : In this Cations becomes excess , In this defect cations from surroundings takes away anions from lattice
    and leaves their electrons there to replace anions.
    Compounds like NaCl and KCl shows this defects in presence of heat and their respective cation vapours.
    Net charge doesnt changes , but density decreases.
    The anion's vacant places are callef F-centers
    Due to this defect , NaCl turns yellow , KCl turns violet ,ZnO turns yellow and LiCl turns pink.



    (b) Metal deficient defect : It can occur when a Metal ion can show multiple valancies , eg Fe+2 being replaced by Fe+3,
    for every 2 Fe+3 one Fe+2 will go missing to maintain net charge in substance.
    This decreases density and changes Molecular formula.
    eg, Fe0.93O means in every 100 Fe+2 sites 7 will be found missing , why that happaned ? Fe+3 replaced them.
